Luminous Infrared Galaxies

Ultra/Luminous infrared galaxies (U/LIRGS) are extraordinary systems that exhibit some of the most extreme star formation in the local universe. The intense star formation is believed to be triggered by the merger of two gas-rich galaxies but the mechanism is not yet well understood. High-resolution carbon monoxide (CO) observations show that they molecular gas is concentrated near the nuclear regions of the systems. Since molecular gas is the fuel for current and future star formation, understanding the physical conditions and distribution of the warm, dense gas in the local U/LIRGS will help in better understanding the details of star formation in the most luminous galaxies and, of course, the history of galaxy formation and evolution.

We use the sample of Wilson et al. (2008) that consists of 14 U/LIRGs observed using the Submillimeter Array (SMA) to study the distribution and kinematics of the molecular gas. The observations are used to constrain the physical conditions such as density and temperature using the radiative transfer code RADEX (van der Tak et al. 2007). RADEX solutions for Arp 299 have shown that the overlap region is similar to giant molecular clouds (GMCs) of the normal spiral galaxy M33 but on a much larger scale (Sliwa et al. 2012). For VV 114, we find an unusually large [12CO]/[13CO] abundance ratio, nearly 3 times as large as the local ISM value (Sliwa et al. 2013).

OH Megamasers

OH megamasers are rare luminous masers that are associated with extreme starburst activity induced by galaxy mergers (Darling 2007). The luminosity of the OH maser emission exceeds that of known galactic masers by a factor of at least 106 (Darling & Giovanelli 2002). Bright 18 cm OH megamaser emission is seen in the nuclei of some ultra/luminous infrared galaxies (U/LIRGs). The standard model has involved a low gain molecular structure on scales of 100-1000 pc amplifying the background continuum (Pihlstrom et al. 2001). The physical conditions that produce the OH megamasers are still not that well understood. These extremely bright masers can be promising tracers of dust-obscured star formation and mergers at high redshifts and could be used to estimate the merger rate as a function of redshift (Darling & Giovanelli 2002). However, in order to use OH megamasers as a high redshift probe we first need to determine what triggers the OH maser emission, for example a specific type or stage of a merger. The maser emission is likely triggered by the physical, chemical and kinematic conditions in the molecular gas in the nuclei.

The sample of Wilson et al. (2008) is best suited to study the conditions that trigger OH megamasers. The sample consists of 5 U/LIRGs with OH megamaser emission and 9 without. Darling (2007) has suggested that U/LIRGS with mean volume densities greater than 103 cm-3 using the Kennicutt-Schmidt law (Krumholz & Thompson 2007). We use the SMA observations to determine the physical conditions of the molecular gas that can trigger OH megamaser emission.
